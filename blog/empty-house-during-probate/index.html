What Happens To An Empty House During Probate?

An empty inherited property can quickly become one of the biggest practical issues during probate. While the estate is being administered, the property may still need insurance, maintenance, security checks and basic running costs.

Insurance and vacant property rules

Standard home insurance may not be suitable if a property is empty for a long period. Executors should check the insurer’s requirements and make sure the estate has appropriate cover.

Council tax and utilities

Even where nobody is living at the property, there may still be council tax, standing charges, utility bills and maintenance costs to manage.

Security and maintenance

Empty homes can be more exposed to leaks, damp, break-ins, garden overgrowth and general deterioration. Regular checks can help protect the estate’s value.

Clearing the property

Many families need time to remove personal belongings. This can be emotionally difficult and may delay a traditional open-market sale.

Why a direct sale can help

Where an inherited property is empty, dated or difficult to manage, a direct sale can reduce holding costs and avoid a long chain or repeated viewings.

  • No need to fully refurbish before sale
  • No estate agent viewings
  • Flexible completion around probate
  • Properties considered with contents or repair issues
A direct sale is not right for every estate, but it can be useful where simplicity and certainty are important.
