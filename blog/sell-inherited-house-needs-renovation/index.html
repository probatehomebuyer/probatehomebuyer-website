Selling an Inherited House That Needs Renovation

Posted 29 May 2026

A practical guide for executors and families deciding whether to refurbish, clear or sell a probate property as it stands.

Probate Advice · Updated May 2026

Should you renovate an inherited house before selling?

Many inherited properties have not been modernised for years. They may need new electrics, heating, roof repairs, damp treatment, clearance, decorating or general refurbishment before they feel ready for the open market.

For some families, spending money before sale can make sense. For others, especially where the property is empty, dated, damaged or far away from the executors, the cost and stress of renovation can outweigh the benefit.

Start with the legal and estate position

Before committing to building works, executors should understand where the estate is in the probate process, who has authority to make decisions and whether all beneficiaries agree with the plan. If a property needs to be sold, the personal representatives usually need the correct authority before completion can take place.

Get realistic costs before starting work

Small improvements can quickly turn into a larger project. A dated inherited house may reveal issues with damp, wiring, plumbing, windows, roof coverings, insulation, asbestos, drainage or structural movement once work starts.

Before renovating, ask whether the likely uplift in sale price is enough to justify the spend, time and risk. A traditional buyer may still reduce their offer after survey, even if cosmetic works have been completed.

Consider holding costs while the work is done

Renovation is not only about the contractor bill. There may also be council tax, insurance, utilities, garden maintenance, security checks, mortgage payments and travel costs while the property remains unsold.

When selling as-is may be better

Selling the property as-is may be more suitable where the house is empty, the estate needs certainty, beneficiaries want a clean break or the executors do not want to manage contractors and viewings.

For probate sellers, the best route is not always the route with the highest theoretical asking price. Certainty, speed and reduced hassle can also be valuable.
